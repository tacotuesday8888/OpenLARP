# OpenLARP Retention Analysis

## Retention Thesis

OpenLARP will retain users only if it becomes part of their identity-change routine.

The app cannot rely on career emergencies alone. Job search tools are often episodic: users come when they need a resume, application tracker, or interview prep, then disappear. OpenLARP needs a more frequent reason to return:

> "I need today's quest because it is the next small step toward becoming the version of myself who can win."

That is a hard behavior to create. Most users want transformation but avoid the tasks that produce it.

## Biggest Retention Risks

### 1. The Product Feels Like ChatGPT With Points

If users can ask ChatGPT, "Make me a career plan," and get a similar result, OpenLARP has weak retention.

Gamification does not solve this. Users will not return daily for XP if the advice is generic.

### 2. The Daily Quest Becomes Homework

Career development is already stressful. If OpenLARP feels like another obligation, users will churn.

Daily quests must feel:

- Specific.
- Small.
- Achievable.
- Emotionally relevant.
- Connected to a visible gap.
- Worth doing even when motivation is low.

### 3. Users Do Not Trust The Diagnosis

"Am I Cooked?" can drive curiosity, but if the result feels random, harsh, or fake, users will not build trust.

Bad diagnostic:

- "You are 62% cooked."

Better diagnostic:

- "You are not cooked, but your proof is weaker than your ambition. Your target roles ask for SQL, dashboards, and stakeholder communication. Your current story only shows coursework."

### 4. The Product Rewards Vanity Progress

XP for opening the app, reading advice, or generating resumes may create surface engagement but not transformation.

If users do not build proof, improve targeting, apply better, or practice interviews, the app becomes a motivational toy.

### 5. Emotional Drop-Off After The First Diagnostic

The diagnostic is likely to be the highest-retention moment and the biggest churn point. Some users will satisfy their curiosity and leave. Others will feel exposed and avoid returning.

The product must immediately convert diagnosis into relief:

> "You are not doomed. Here is today's small action."

### 6. Job Search Is Rejection-Heavy

Users may associate OpenLARP with rejection if applications do not convert quickly.

The product should track controllable progress, not only outcomes:

- Better target clarity.
- Better proof.
- Better outreach.
- Better interview answers.
- Better application quality.

### 7. Target Users Have Weak Daily Discipline

Students and new grads often have inconsistent schedules. Ambitious professionals are busy. Career switchers may be emotionally exhausted.

The app must assume low discipline and design for re-entry after missed days.

### 8. AI Output May Feel Generic Or Inaccurate

Career advice must be grounded in the user's actual background. Generic AI plans destroy confidence.

### 9. Users May Be Embarrassed

Career insecurity is private. Users may not want to share progress, streaks, or identity aspirations publicly.

Social features should be optional and likely private-first.

### 10. The Product May Attract Users Who Want Shortcuts

The slogan "Fake it till you make it - honestly" is strong but risky. Some users may interpret it as help with faking credibility.

The product must clearly reject lying and steer users toward proof-building.

## Strongest Habit Loops

### Habit Loop 1: Anxiety -> Daily Quest -> Relief

Trigger:

- "I am behind."
- "I do not know what to do next."
- "Everyone else seems ahead."

Action:

- Open OpenLARP.
- Complete one small quest.

Reward:

- Relief.
- Sense of control.
- Visible gap shrink.

Risk:

- If quests are too hard, anxiety increases.

### Habit Loop 2: Identity Gap -> Proof Creation -> Level Up

Trigger:

- "I want to become a product manager / data analyst / designer / founder / engineer."

Action:

- Complete a proof-building task.

Reward:

- The user's profile becomes more credible.
- Their "future self" feels less fictional.

Risk:

- Requires thoughtful personalization.

### Habit Loop 3: Streak -> Accountability -> Momentum

Trigger:

- Daily reminder.
- Streak at risk.

Action:

- Complete or defer a quest.

Reward:

- Maintain streak.
- Unlock weekly report.

Risk:

- Streak loss can cause churn. The app needs forgiveness mechanics.

### Habit Loop 4: Weekly Level-Up Report

Trigger:

- End of week.

Action:

- Review progress.

Reward:

- "Here is how you are less cooked than last week."
- Clear next mission.

Risk:

- Reports must be concrete, not motivational filler.

### Habit Loop 5: Real-World Event -> OpenLARP Prep

Trigger:

- Interview scheduled.
- Career fair.
- Application deadline.
- Recruiter message.
- New target role.

Action:

- Open app for a specific mission.

Reward:

- Preparedness.
- Confidence.

Risk:

- Episodic rather than daily retention.

### Habit Loop 6: Personal Score Movement

Trigger:

- User sees a measurable gap.

Action:

- Complete a task that moves one gap.

Reward:

- Proof gap improves from "weak" to "developing."
- Interview readiness improves after recorded practice.

Risk:

- Scores must be explainable and tied to behavior.

## Reasons Users Would Quit

- The plan feels generic.
- The app nags without helping.
- Quests are too big.
- Quests are too trivial.
- The product feels childish.
- The diagnostic feels mean.
- The diagnostic feels fake.
- Progress does not connect to jobs or outcomes.
- Users get rejected and blame the app.
- Users do not want career help daily.
- Users prefer ChatGPT because it is flexible.
- Users prefer LinkedIn because it has jobs and recruiters.
- Users prefer Teal, Huntr, or Careerflow because they manage applications directly.
- Users feel guilty after breaking a streak.
- Users feel manipulated by gamification.
- Users do not want to upload sensitive career data.
- Users want auto-apply shortcuts, which OpenLARP should avoid.

## Reasons Users Would Return

- They trust the app's diagnosis.
- They believe the plan understands their real situation.
- Today's quest is specific and doable.
- The app gives emotional relief.
- Progress is visible.
- The user sees their resume, profile, portfolio, network, or interview answers improving.
- The app helps them act when they feel stuck.
- The weekly report makes growth tangible.
- The app remembers context better than ChatGPT.
- The app creates accountability without shame.
- The app offers clear next steps after rejection.
- The app turns vague ambition into daily execution.

## Why Daily Return Is Hard

Career development does not naturally require daily app use. People may need career support intensely for a few weeks, then not at all for months.

To earn daily usage, OpenLARP must become less like a document tool and more like a guided training program.

The daily visit must answer:

> "What is the one thing I should do today that future me will thank me for?"

If OpenLARP cannot answer that better than ChatGPT, it will not retain.

## Retention Design Principles

### 1. One Action Per Day

Do not overwhelm users with a dashboard full of tools. Give one main action.

### 2. Show Why This Task Matters

Every quest should connect to a gap:

- "This reduces your proof gap."
- "This improves your networking surface."
- "This makes your interview story more credible."

### 3. Make Missed Days Recoverable

Streaks should bend, not break.

Possible mechanics:

- Recovery quest.
- Weekend catch-up.
- Streak freeze earned by proof, not purchased.
- "Resume the campaign" button.

### 4. Reward Real Proof

XP should be tied to proof:

- Pasted message sent.
- Project link.
- Resume bullet improved.
- Interview recording completed.
- Job target list refined.

Avoid rewarding passive app use.

### 5. Keep The Tone Honest

The product can be playful, but it should not flatter falsely.

Bad:

- "You are crushing it!"

Better:

- "You completed the first proof task. Your story is still thin, but now it has one concrete example."

### 6. Create Emotional Safety

The user should feel challenged but not humiliated.

"Am I Cooked?" should become "Here is the way out."

## Recommended Retention Experiments

### Experiment 1: Daily Quest Completion

Test whether users complete one quest per day for 14 days.

Success:

- 50% complete day 1.
- 30% return day 7.
- 20% complete at least 7 quests.

### Experiment 2: Diagnostic Tone

A/B test:

- Playful "Am I Cooked?" tone.
- Calm professional "Career Gap Audit" tone.
- Hybrid tone.

Measure:

- Diagnostic completion.
- Day 1 quest start.
- Day 3 return.
- Qualitative trust.

### Experiment 3: Proof-Based XP

Compare:

- XP for checking off tasks.
- XP only after submitting proof.

Measure:

- Completion quality.
- Retention.
- User frustration.

### Experiment 4: Reminder Channel

Compare:

- Push-style SMS.
- Email.
- Discord.
- Calendar task.

Measure:

- Open rate.
- Quest completion.
- Churn from annoyance.

### Experiment 5: Human Review vs AI Only

Compare:

- Fully automated feedback.
- Founder-reviewed weekly report.

Measure:

- Willingness to pay.
- Trust.
- Repeat sprint requests.

### Experiment 6: Quest Difficulty

Compare:

- 10-minute micro-quests.
- 30-minute standard quests.
- 60-minute deep-work missions.

Measure:

- Completion rate.
- Perceived value.
- Retention.

### Experiment 7: Outcome Reframing After Rejection

When users report rejection, give:

- A normal message.
- A post-rejection mission.
- A targeting recalibration.

Measure:

- Return after rejection.
- Emotional trust.

### Experiment 8: Gap Shrink Visualization

Compare:

- XP/levels only.
- Gap bars only.
- XP plus gap bars.

Measure:

- User comprehension.
- Motivation.
- Day 7 return.

## Retention Verdict

OpenLARP's retention risk is high.

The strongest path is not more features. It is a daily proof-building loop that makes users feel less stuck and more credible.

If the product becomes a generic career toolbox, retention will be weak. If it becomes a personalized identity-training system with honest progress, retention may be strong enough to justify building the full iOS app.
